## 1. Loop by TerraCycle

### Overview
Launched in 2019 at the World Economic Forum in Davos. An e-commerce and retail reusable container platform partnering with major CPG brands. Shut down e-commerce operations in most markets by 2023; surviving only in France at industrial scale through Carrefour.

### Brand Partners
Procter & Gamble, Nestle (founding investors), PepsiCo, Unilever, Mars Petcare, Clorox, The Body Shop, Coca-Cola European Partners, Mondelez International, Danone.

### Container Specifications

| Attribute | Details |
|---|---|
| **Materials** | Material-agnostic: stainless steel, glass, aluminum, engineered durable plastics. "So long as the material is durable, can be refilled, and survive the rigors of the platform." |
| **Rated reuse cycles** | 100+ uses per container |
| **Example: Haagen-Dazs ice cream** | Double-walled stainless steel canister (keeps ice cream frozen for hours) |
| **Example: Pantene shampoo** | Lightweight durable aluminum bottle |
| **Example: Crest mouthwash** | Glass bottle with silicone sleeve and stainless steel cap |
| **Example: Signal toothpaste** | Refillable glass container (for tablet format) |
| **Deposit range** | $1 to $10 per container depending on size and material |
| **Shipping container** | Reusable tote bag ($15 deposit) |
| **Specific dimensions** | Not publicly standardized; each brand designed its own container to Loop specs |
| **Tracking method** | Centralized logistics; UPS pickup scheduling; no per-container electronic tracking reported |
| **Wash method** | Industrial cleaning at TerraCycle facilities before refilling |

### How Returns Worked
- E-commerce model: Customer accumulates empties, schedules UPS pickup in the reusable tote bag, containers returned to cleaning/refilling facility.
- Retail model (later pivot): In-store return at partner retailers (Carrefour in France, Tesco in UK, Kroger/Walmart in US).

### What Failed and Why

**1. Price premium was severe:**
- Products cost significantly more: 1.8 oz jar of cumin = $10; 16 oz nut butter = $14.
- Deposits added 20-25% to order totals (one reviewer paid $30.50 in deposits on a single order, ~23% of total).
- Shipping fees of $10-$20 on top of product prices and deposits.

**2. Logistics friction:**
- Customers had to schedule UPS pickups to return empties (not on-demand).
- Bulky reusable tote was problematic for city-dwellers in small apartments.
- Customers had to decide whether they had accumulated enough empties to justify a return pickup.

**3. Consumer behavior change too steep:**
- Required entirely new shopping habits with no convenience advantage over disposable.
- Tesco UK cited "implementation challenges" and need for "cultural and behavioural shift."
- 80,000 Loop products sold in UK over two years before Tesco pulled out.

**4. Scale problem:**
- Small-scale pilots were "almost intrinsically set to fail" (Tom Szaky, CEO).
- Shoppers "either want to go all-in, or not go there at all."

**5. Why France succeeded:**
- Carrefour committed to rapid, large-scale rollout (340+ supermarkets, 370+ product lines).
- Loop products in France are priced LESS than disposable equivalents (including deposit).
- Best-performing categories: beverages (beer, soda, water) with short consumption cycles and fast container turnover.
- Reached profitability and industrial scale.

**6. Greenwashing lawsuit:**
- Last Beach Clean Up filed a lawsuit in 2020 alleging TerraCycle was not telling the full truth about recyclability.

### Key Lessons
- Price parity or advantage is non-negotiable for mass adoption.
- Return logistics must be frictionless (in-store return >> scheduled pickup).
- Scale from day one; small pilots don't generate enough network effect.
- Fast-turnover products (beverages) work better than slow-turnover (household cleaning).

## 2. RECUP / REBOWL (Germany)

### Overview
Founded September 2016 in Munich by Fabian Eckert and Florian Pachaly. Largest returnable cup/bowl system in Germany. Operates a deposit-based model with no app registration required.

### Container Specifications

| Attribute | RECUP (Cups) | REBOWL (Bowls) |
|---|---|---|
| **Material** | Polypropylene (PP) | Polypropylene (PP) |
| **BPA-free** | Yes | Yes |
| **Sizes** | 0.2L, 0.3L, 0.4L, 0.5L | Multiple sizes (not publicly specified in mm) |
| **Heat resistance** | Suitable for hot beverages | Up to 90 degrees C |
| **Leak-proof** | N/A (open cup) | Yes |
| **Stackable** | Yes | Yes |
| **Rated reuse cycles** | 200-500 uses (up to 1,000 disposable cup replacements) | 200-500 uses (up to 500 disposable replacements) |
| **Manufactured** | Germany | Germany |
| **Specific dimensions (mm)** | Not publicly listed | Not publicly listed |
| **Weight (g)** | Not publicly listed | Not publicly listed |

### Deposit Model
- **RECUP deposit:** EUR 1
- **REBOWL deposit:** EUR 5
- No app registration required; no personal data needed.
- Borrow at any partner, return at any partner.
- Network: 13,000+ distribution and return points across Germany (cafes, restaurants, bakeries, kiosks, gas stations, company canteens).

### Wash Method
- Containers washed by the food service partner (restaurant, cafe) using their existing commercial dishwashers.
- No centralized washing facility; distributed wash model.
- Containers are PP and rated for commercial dishwasher cycles.

### Tracking Method
- Physical deposit token model (no electronic tracking per container).
- System relies on deposit incentive rather than digital tracking.

### McDonald's Germany Note
McDonald's Germany developed its own nationwide reusable cup deposit system (with HAVI logistics) rather than joining RECUP. This fragmentation of standards is a known challenge.

### Reported Problems
- Fragmentation: McDonald's, Burger King, and RECUP all operate separate incompatible systems.
- Germany's January 2023 reusable mandate (Mehrwegangebotspflicht) requires all food service businesses selling to-go food/drink to offer a reusable option, but enforcement and adoption remain inconsistent.
- No universal standard across systems means consumers face confusion about where to return what.

## 3. Vytal (Germany)

### Overview
Digital (app-based), deposit-free reusable food container system. Active in Germany and Austria. Partners with restaurants, canteens, and delivery services. No upfront deposit; instead, containers must be returned within 14 days or a fee is charged.

### Container Specifications

| Container Type | Volume | Material | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| Small Bowl | 500 mL | Polypropylene (PP), BPA-free | Leak-proof, heat-resistant, heat-insulating, aroma-proof |
| Medium Bowl | 750 mL | Polypropylene (PP), BPA-free | Same as above |
| Large Bowl | 1,250 mL | Polypropylene (PP), BPA-free | Same as above |
| Compartment Bowl | 1,000 mL total | Polypropylene (PP), BPA-free | Divided sections for meal components |
| Coffee Cup (small) | 200 mL | Polypropylene (PP), BPA-free | With drinking lid |
| Coffee Cup (medium) | 300 mL | Polypropylene (PP), BPA-free | With drinking lid |
| Coffee Cup (large) | 400 mL | Polypropylene (PP), BPA-free | With drinking lid |
| Long Drink Cup | 500 mL | Polypropylene (PP), BPA-free | Heat-resistant to 85 degrees C |
| Sushi Container | 500 mL | 75% recycled stainless steel + Tritan plastic | NOT microwaveable |
| Pizza Container | Fits up to 33 cm diameter | Polypropylene (PP), BPA-free | Innovative reusable pizza box |

| Attribute | Details |
|---|---|
| **Specific dimensions (mm)** | Not publicly listed (available in downloadable product portfolio PDF) |
| **Weight (g)** | Not publicly listed |
| **Dishwasher safe** | Yes, domestic and industrial |
| **Microwave safe** | Yes (except sushi container; lids should not be microwaved) |
| **Freezer safe** | Yes |
| **Rated reuse cycles** | 200+ uses per container |
| **Manufactured in** | Odenwald, Germany |

### Tracking Method
- **App-based QR code tracking.** Each container has a unique QR code.
- At pickup: restaurant scans QR codes on containers and in customer's app, or customer self-scans.
- App shows return deadline and nearby return locations.
- Reported return rate: **99.2%**
- Average return time: **< 5 days**

### Business Model
- No consumer deposit. Instead: 14-day return window; fee charged if not returned.
- Restaurants pay ~EUR 300 per 500 container uses.
- Containers also accepted back via delivery services.

### Reported Problems / Challenges
- Geographic limitation: works well locally but difficult when traveling (need a Vytal return point at destination).
- Incompatible with RECUP and other systems (different technology, different containers).
- Consumer awareness and default adoption remain challenges.
- Political lobbying required to make reusable the default rather than opt-in.

## 4. CupClub / CLUBZERO (UK)

### Overview
Founded in London by Safia Qureshi. Originally called CupClub; rebranded to CLUBZERO. Won the Ellen MacArthur Foundation Design Challenge. Provides "cups as a service" for office buildings, cafes, and corporate campuses.

### Container Specifications

| Attribute | Details |
|---|---|
| **Cup material** | Injection-moulded polypropylene (PP) |
| **Cup weight** | 49.3 g |
| **Lid material** | Low-density polyethylene (LDPE) |
| **Lid weight** | 22.03 g |
| **Total weight (cup + lid)** | ~71.4 g |
| **RFID tag weight** | < 0.1 g |
| **Suitable for** | Hot and cold drinks |
| **Rated reuse cycles** | 132 uses, then recycled |
| **Specific dimensions (mm)** | Not publicly listed; designed to match standard disposable cup sizes |
| **Tracking method** | RFID chip embedded in every cup |
| **Environmental claim** | 50% less CO2 vs. disposable cups; 40% less single-use plastic |

### How It Works
- Cups distributed at partner cafes/offices.
- After use, customer drops cup at designated collection points.
- Collected cups are taken to a centralized washing facility.
- Cleaned cups are redistributed to outlets.
- RFID chip enables tracking of every cup's location and lifecycle stage.

### Reported Problems
- Limited to closed-loop environments (office buildings, campuses) where collection points can be managed.
- Scaling to open urban environments is much harder.
- 132-cycle lifespan is relatively low compared to German systems (200-500 cycles).

## 5. Returnity

### Overview
US-based company producing reusable shipping containers for retail and e-commerce logistics. Flagship product: "The Last Box."

### Container Specifications

| Attribute | Details |
|---|---|
| **Product name** | The Last Box |
| **Form factor** | Durable, collapsible, tapeless tote |
| **Material** | Lightweight composite (likely woven polypropylene; exact material not publicly disclosed) |
| **Weight capacity** | 50+ lbs (22+ kg) per box |
| **Rated reuse cycles** | 50+ shipment cycles |
| **Collapsibility** | 10 folded units nest into 1 for backhaul |
| **Palletization** | 30 Last Boxes per pallet |
| **Specific dimensions (mm)** | Not publicly listed |
| **Water resistance** | Yes |
| **Tamper resistance** | Yes |
| **Tracking method** | Not specified in public materials |
| **Environmental claim** | 30% packaging cost reduction per cycle; up to 88% carbon reduction |

### Use Cases
- Internal distribution center to store transfers.
- Pick-and-pack for curbside/local delivery.
- Outbound transfers and returns.
- Designed to work within existing logistics infrastructure (no system redesign needed).

### Key Design Insight
- The critical innovation is **compatibility with existing logistics systems** -- fits on standard pallets, works with automated sorting, uses existing courier networks. This is a major lesson for any reusable container system.

## 6. Standard European Refrigerator Dimensions

### External Dimensions (Standard 60cm European Fridge)

| Attribute | Typical Range |
|---|---|
| **External width** | 540-600 mm |
| **External depth** | 550-650 mm |
| **External height** | 1,470-1,820 mm (fridge-freezer combo) |

### Internal Usable Shelf Dimensions (Derived)

Exact internal dimensions vary by manufacturer and model. However, based on engineering standards for 60cm-wide European fridges:

| Attribute | Estimated Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Internal cavity width** | 470-520 mm | After insulation walls (~40-50 mm total) |
| **Usable shelf width** | 450-500 mm | Shelves slightly narrower than cavity |
| **Usable shelf depth** | 350-430 mm | Front-to-back; varies with door shelf intrusion |
| **Shelf spacing (vertical)** | 100-200 mm typical | Adjustable; most common setting ~150 mm |
| **Door shelf depth** | 80-120 mm | For bottles and small containers |

### Critical Design Constraint
**A reusable container for domestic use must fit within ~450-500 mm width and ~350-400 mm depth to be universally compatible with standard European 60cm fridges.** Containers should also be stackable and ideally no taller than 100-150 mm to allow stacking on a single shelf.

### Compatibility with Gastronorm Sizes
- **GN 1/4 (265 x 163 mm):** Fits easily; two can sit side-by-side (330 mm total width).
- **GN 1/2 (325 x 265 mm):** Fits in most fridges with room to spare.
- **GN 1/3 (325 x 176 mm):** Fits; good for narrow items.
- **GN 1/1 (530 x 325 mm):** TOO WIDE for most domestic fridges. Not viable for home use.

## 8. Gastronorm (GN) Container Standard

### Overview
The Gastronorm system (EN 631 standard) was introduced in Switzerland in 1964 and became an official European standard in 1993. It is the universal modular sizing system used in every commercial kitchen, food service trolley, blast chiller, combi oven, and bain-marie in Europe.

### Complete GN Size Chart

| GN Size | Dimensions (L x W mm) | Relationship to GN 1/1 |
|---|---|---|
| **GN 2/1** | 650 x 530 | Double full-size |
| **GN 1/1** | 530 x 325 | Full-size (base unit) |
| **GN 2/3** | 354 x 325 | Two-thirds |
| **GN 2/4** | 530 x 162 | Two-quarters (long narrow) |
| **GN 2/8** | 325 x 132 | Two-eighths |
| **GN 1/2** | 325 x 265 | Half |
| **GN 1/3** | 325 x 176 | Third |
| **GN 1/4** | 265 x 163 | Quarter |
| **GN 1/6** | 176 x 162 | Sixth |
| **GN 1/9** | 108 x 176 | Ninth |

### Standard Depths
10 mm, 20 mm, 40 mm, 65 mm, 100 mm, 150 mm, 200 mm.

Most common general-purpose depth: **65 mm**.

### Approximate Capacities (GN 1/1 reference)

| Depth | GN 1/1 Capacity |
|---|---|
| 20 mm | ~1.0 L |
| 40 mm | ~2.5 L |
| 65 mm | ~4.0 L |
| 100 mm | ~7.0 L |
| 150 mm | ~12.0 L |
| 200 mm | ~16.0 L |

### Materials Available
- **AISI 304 stainless steel** -- most common, most durable, most expensive
- **Polycarbonate (clear)** -- allows content visibility, lighter weight
- **Polypropylene** -- lightweight, cheaper, dishwasher-safe, microwave-safe
- **Melamine** -- for display/service use
- **Enameled steel** -- specialty applications
- **Porcelain/earthenware** -- service/presentation

### Why GN Matters for a Reusable Container System

**The GN standard is the strongest candidate for a base sizing system because:**

1. **Universal infrastructure compatibility.** Every commercial kitchen, trolley, chiller, and oven in Europe already accepts GN containers. A reusable system using GN-compatible sizing would instantly plug into existing food service infrastructure.

2. **Domestic fridge compatibility.** GN 1/4 (265 x 163 mm) and GN 1/2 (325 x 265 mm) fit comfortably in standard European fridges.

3. **Dishwasher compatibility.** GN 1/4 and smaller fit easily in both domestic and commercial dishwasher racks. Multiple GN 1/4 containers fit in a single 500 x 500 mm commercial rack.

4. **Supply chain exists.** GN containers in stainless steel and PP are mass-produced globally at commodity prices.

5. **Stackability is built in.** GN containers are designed to stack precisely, with lids, in trolleys and storage.

### Recommended GN Sizes for a Reusable Consumer System

| Use Case | Recommended GN Size | Dimensions | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Single meal portion | GN 1/4, 65 mm deep | 265 x 163 x 65 mm | ~1.8 L; fits in fridge, dishwasher, hand |
| Family/sharing portion | GN 1/2, 65 mm deep | 325 x 265 x 65 mm | ~4.0 L; fits in fridge |
| Small side/sauce | GN 1/6, 65 mm deep | 176 x 162 x 65 mm | ~1.0 L; compact |
| Deep storage/soup | GN 1/4, 100 mm deep | 265 x 163 x 100 mm | ~2.5 L; still fits in fridge |
| Salad/large item | GN 1/3, 65 mm deep | 325 x 176 x 65 mm | ~2.5 L; long format |

## 9. Industrial CIP (Clean-in-Place) Wash Requirements

### Overview
CIP is the standard methodology for cleaning food-contact equipment and containers at scale without disassembly. For a reusable container depot, a modified CIP/tunnel wash approach would be used.

### Standard CIP Cycle (5 Steps)

| Step | Temperature | Duration | Chemical | Concentration |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Pre-rinse | 32-43 degrees C (cool water) | 3-5 min | None (water only) | N/A |
| 2. Caustic wash | 60-80 degrees C | 15-30 min | Sodium hydroxide (NaOH) | 0.5-2.0% (up to 4% for heavy soil) |
| 3. Intermediate rinse | Ambient-warm | 3-5 min | None (water only) | N/A |
| 4. Acid wash | 60-70 degrees C | 10-20 min | Nitric acid (HNO3) | 0.5% typical |
| 5. Final rinse/sanitize | 85-95 degrees C | 3-5 min | None (hot water sanitization) or chemical sanitizer | N/A |

**Total cycle time: ~35-65 minutes for full CIP.**

### Commercial Dishwasher Approach (More Relevant for Container Washing)

For washing discrete containers (not piping/tanks), commercial warewashing machines are more appropriate than CIP:

| Parameter | Specification |
|---|---|
| **Wash temperature** | 60-65 degrees C |
| **Rinse temperature** | 80-85 degrees C (DIN 10534 / NSF ANSI-3 compliant) |
| **Cycle time (undercounter)** | 80-95 seconds per rack |
| **Cycle time (rack conveyor/tunnel)** | 45-163 seconds per rack |
| **Throughput (tunnel)** | 90-280 trolleys/hour (MEIKO BA 251-C / BA 252-C) |
| **Detergent** | Commercial alkaline warewash detergent |
| **Rinse aid** | Commercial rinse aid for spot-free drying |
| **Standard rack size** | 500 x 500 mm |

### Key Equipment Manufacturers
- **MEIKO** (Germany) -- market leader in commercial warewashing; makes tunnel washers, trolley washers, rack conveyors.
- **Winterhalter** (Germany) -- premium commercial dishwashers; UC-L model noted for double-walled construction, water efficiency.
- **Hobart** -- global commercial warewashing.

### Container Material Requirements for Industrial Washing

| Material | Industrial Wash Compatible? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Polypropylene (PP) | Yes | Withstands 85-95 degrees C rinse; resistant to NaOH and mild acids |
| Stainless steel 304 | Yes | Best durability; withstands all chemicals and temperatures |
| Polycarbonate | Caution | Can cloud/crack with repeated high-temp alkaline washing |
| Glass | Yes | Durable but heavy and breakage risk |
| Tritan | Yes | Good chemical resistance; lighter than glass |
| LDPE | Limited | Softens at high temperatures; not ideal for repeated industrial wash |

## 10. Cross-System Comparison & Lessons Learned

### Comparative Summary

| System | Material | Tracking | Deposit? | Reuse Cycles | Wash Model | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Loop** | Glass, steel, aluminum, plastics | Centralized logistics | Yes ($1-$10) | 100+ | Centralized facility | Failed in most markets; surviving in France |
| **RECUP/REBOWL** | Polypropylene | None (deposit token) | Yes (EUR 1/5) | 200-500 | Distributed (each restaurant) | Active; 13,000+ points in Germany |
| **Vytal** | Polypropylene | App + QR code | No deposit (14-day return) | 200+ | Distributed (each restaurant) | Active; 99.2% return rate |
| **CupClub/CLUBZERO** | Polypropylene | RFID chip | No deposit | 132 | Centralized wash facility | Active in closed environments (offices) |
| **Returnity** | Woven composite | Not specified | N/A (B2B) | 50+ | N/A (shipping, not food) | Active; B2B logistics |

### Consolidated Lessons Learned

**1. Price must be at parity or cheaper than disposable.**
Loop failed in every market where reusable products cost more. It only succeeded in France when prices were set below disposable equivalents. This is the single most critical factor.

**2. Returns must be effortless.**
- Best: return anywhere in a large network (RECUP: 13,000+ points).
- Good: app-guided return with 14-day window (Vytal).
- Bad: schedule a courier pickup (Loop e-commerce).
- Return density (number of return points per km2) directly predicts system viability.

**3. Distributed washing beats centralized washing for food containers.**
- RECUP and Vytal push washing to the restaurant (which already has a commercial dishwasher). This eliminates reverse logistics for dirty containers.
- Loop and CupClub centralize washing, which adds transportation cost, carbon, and complexity.
- Exception: centralized washing makes sense for high-volume closed loops (office campuses, stadiums).

**4. Polypropylene is the dominant material.**
- Every active system (RECUP, Vytal, CupClub) uses PP.
- PP is lightweight, dishwasher-safe (domestic and commercial), microwave-safe, BPA-free, cheap to mold, and survives 200-500 cycles.
- Stainless steel is more durable but heavier and more expensive; justified only for premium applications (Vytal sushi container).
- Glass is heavy, fragile, and expensive; Loop's glass containers contributed to logistics costs.

**5. Tracking must match the use case.**
- Deposit model (RECUP): no tracking needed; deposit incentive is sufficient. Simple, no app required. But no data on container lifecycle.
- App + QR (Vytal): enables 99.2% return rate, lifecycle tracking, and user reminders. Requires smartphone and willingness to scan. Generates valuable data.
- RFID (CupClub): most sophisticated tracking but adds hardware cost (~$0.10+ per tag) and requires reader infrastructure.

**6. Standardization is critical.**
- Germany now has multiple incompatible reusable systems (RECUP, Vytal, McDonald's own system, Burger King's own system). Consumers are confused about what goes where.
- A GN-compatible sizing standard would avoid this fragmentation by building on an existing universal standard.

**7. Scale from day one.**
- Loop's biggest lesson: small pilots are "almost intrinsically set to fail."
- Carrefour's France rollout (340+ stores immediately) worked. Tesco's cautious UK pilot did not.
- The network effect matters: more return points mean more consumer convenience, which means higher return rates, which means lower costs.

**8. Fast-turnover products work best.**
- Beverages (beer, soda, water) had the highest success rate in Loop France because containers cycle back quickly.
- Slow-turnover products (cleaning supplies, personal care) mean containers sit in homes for weeks/months, reducing system efficiency.

**9. GN-compatible sizing is the strongest foundation for a new system.**
- GN containers already fit in every commercial kitchen, combi oven, blast chiller, and food trolley in Europe.
- GN 1/4 and GN 1/2 fit in domestic fridges and dishwashers.
- Adopting GN-compatible dimensions means the reusable system works with existing infrastructure on both the commercial and domestic sides, eliminating the need for custom equipment.
- Polypropylene GN containers are already mass-produced at commodity scale.
